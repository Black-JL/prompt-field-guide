The frame A prompt isn't a question — it's a program. You're programming a prediction engine with words. The more structure you give it, the better it predicts what you actually want. — Jules White, Vanderbilt

CO-STAR-E

Six letters plus an example. The same instinct as an OPORD or an SBAR — a checklist so you stop forgetting the parts that matter. Use every letter, including the ones you're tempted to skip.

C

Context

The background the AI needs to answer well. Who's asking, what's the setting, what's already true.

"Night-shift charge nurse at a Level II trauma center, 14 beds occupied, two new admits…"
O

Objective

The actual ask. The verb. What you want the AI to do — specifically, not generally.

"Draft an SBAR handoff note for the oncoming shift."
S

Style

How it should read. Structure, length, formatting, vocabulary. The shape of the output.

"Bullet points under each SBAR heading. Under 200 words. Active voice."
T

Tone (or Persona)

The emotional register. Clinical or warm. Direct or diplomatic. Hedged or committed.

"Clinical. Direct. No hedging unless the data genuinely supports it."
A

Audience

Who's reading. A patient. A specialist. A coder. A commander. Each pulls a different vocabulary.

"Oncoming charge nurse. Assume clinical fluency. No abbreviations they wouldn't know."
R

Response

The format you want back. Paragraph. Table. JSON. Email. SBAR. A structured object you can paste straight into the chart.

"Output: four SBAR sections, one bullet list per section. Plain text. No preamble."
E

Example

Show, don't tell. One or two examples of the kind of answer you want — often beats a paragraph of rules.

"Here's a handoff that worked: [paste]. Match this voice and structure."

CO-STAR-E adapted from Sheila Teo — winner of GovTech Singapore's first GPT-4 prompt-engineering competition (2023). The "E" is our addition.

Copy-paste templates

Paste this skeleton into any chat and fill the brackets. Labeling each section is the point — it forces you to supply every part.

# CONTEXT
[Who you are, the setting, what's already true.]

# OBJECTIVE
[The specific task. Use a verb — draft, summarize, compare, rewrite.]

# STYLE
[Structure, length, formatting, vocabulary.]

# TONE
[Clinical / warm. Direct / diplomatic. Hedged / committed.]

# AUDIENCE
[Who reads this, and what vocabulary they expect.]

# RESPONSE
[The exact output format you want back.]

# EXAMPLE
[Paste one example of a good answer: "Match this voice and structure."]

The same prompt, worked through — the SBAR handoff from the lab:

# CONTEXT
Night-shift charge nurse at a Level II trauma center, 14 beds occupied, two new admits.

# OBJECTIVE
Draft an SBAR handoff note for the oncoming shift.

# STYLE
Bullet points under each SBAR heading. Under 200 words. Active voice.

# TONE
Clinical. Direct. No hedging unless the data genuinely supports it.

# AUDIENCE
Oncoming charge nurse. Assume clinical fluency. No abbreviations they wouldn't know.

# RESPONSE
Four SBAR sections, one bullet list per section. Plain text. No preamble.

# EXAMPLE
Here's a handoff that worked: [paste]. Match this voice and structure.
Before you paste a real example De-identify it first. See What not to paste.

RCO — when CO-STAR-E is too much

The minimum viable prompt. If you only have time for three letters, use these: Role, Context, Output. A specific role activates the right vocabulary; context fills gaps the model would otherwise invent; output saves you reformatting.

ROLE:    You are a [specific role — e.g., a clinical pharmacist].
CONTEXT: [The setting, the data, the constraints it needs to know.]
OUTPUT:  [Format, length, structure you want back.]

"You are a clinical pharmacist" beats "you are an AI assistant." Specificity over generality.

Add reasoning

Bolt this onto any prompt — a full CO-STAR-E prompt or a one-liner. Accuracy goes up and the reasoning becomes auditable, so you can see where it went wrong.

Walk me through your reasoning step by step, then conclude.

Creative · Balanced · Precise

The toggle behind the chat box is "temperature" — how widely the model samples its next word. High is creative and surprising; low is precise and predictable. When your tool hides the dial, simulate it in words:

Be precise. Don't speculate.            ← low temperature
Give me five different framings.        ← high temperature

Three failure modes to guard against

These are the ways a confident-sounding answer is wrong. Each has a tell and a fix.

1 · Confidently plausible — hallucinated facts

Citations, DOIs, drug interactions, and regulations are tokens to predict, not facts to retrieve. Plausible-looking is easier than true — and the format is so clean that readers stop checking. Ask a no-retrieval model for "three peer-reviewed papers with DOIs" and you'll get references that look perfect and don't exist.

Fix Never trust an AI citation, number, or name you haven't clicked through and verified at the source. And explicitly give the model permission to say "I don't know" — Anthropic's simplest hallucination-reduction tip.
If you are not certain, say "I don't know" rather than guessing.
For any citation, give only sources you are confident exist, and
flag anything you are inferring rather than recalling.

2 · The agreement machine — sycophancy

Models are trained to be agreeable. Bake your conclusion into the prompt and it will defend it — then defend the opposite premise just as confidently. "Don't you agree X?" gets you a confirmation machine.

Fix Strip your conclusion out of the prompt. Ask the neutral question instead:
What does the evidence say about [topic]? Give me the strongest
case on each side, then tell me where the weight of evidence falls.

Documented at scale in Sharma et al. (2023), Towards Understanding Sycophancy in Language Models.

3 · It picks, it won't tell you — silent constraint-dropping

When your instructions contradict each other — "two paragraphs" and "bullets throughout," "all 12 recommendations" and "under 100 words" — the model silently chooses which to honor and drops the rest. No question, no flag, just something that looks like an answer.

Fix Audit your own prompt for contradictions before sending, then check the output against your constraint list item by item. The AI fails silently; you have to check loudly. You can also make it do the audit:
Before you answer: list any constraints in my request that conflict
with each other. If there are conflicts, ask me which to prioritize
instead of guessing.

The broader instruction-following failure is measured by IFEval (Zhou et al., 2023).

What not to paste

Protect the data
  • PHI — patient names, MRNs, DOBs, addresses, photos, anything that identifies a patient.
  • PII — SSN, DOD ID, full names paired with sensitive data.
  • CUI — controlled unclassified info: operational details, readiness data, anything marked.
De-identify before pasting: "67-year-old male, CHF" is fine; "John Smith, MRN 4827193" is not. If it's official Army work, route to Ask Sage / Army Enterprise LLM Workspace — not Copilot or ChatGPT.

When to start over

If the AI is forgetting things, repeating itself, drifting off-topic, or contradicting earlier answers, you've saturated the context window. The fix is one click: a new chat is a clean slate. Summarize the prior thread in two sentences and start fresh. (Free consumer Gemini's ~32K-token window fills in about a 20-minute session.)

The context window

The "prompt" is only the visible tip. Below the waterline, the model also sees the system prompt, the full conversation history, attached files, tool outputs, retrieved documents, memory, and the budget reserved for its answer — all of it competing for a finite number of tokens. When the window fills, the oldest material falls off first.

The rule — ABC Always Be Contexting. Never make the model fill in a gap you could have filled yourself. Curate what's below the waterline; don't dump. The smallest possible set of high-signal tokens wins.

Use AI to evaluate AI

Run the same task two or three ways, then paste all of them into a fresh chat — ideally a different AI than the one that wrote them — and have it compare. This is itself a CO-STAR-E prompt: the framework all the way down.

# CONTEXT
You are a critical reader evaluating several drafts of the same task.
The drafts are prompts and their outputs, written by me.

# OBJECTIVE
Compare and contrast the rounds. For each, name what worked, what
didn't, and the biggest change from the round before.

# RESPONSE
A short table: Round | What worked | What didn't | Biggest improvement.
Then one paragraph: which round you'd ship, and why.

# DATA
[paste Round 1 prompt + output]
[paste Round 2 prompt + output]
[paste Round 3 prompt + output]

Think first, prompt second

The AI can only be as clear as you are. Describe what you want before you open the AI, then red-team your own description — attack the gaps — and only then write the prompt.

Two cautions worth keeping "If you can't explain it clearly, you can't prompt it." — Daniel Miessler
"Using AI for your homework is like driving your car for exercise." — Po-Shen Loh, CMU. Generate ideas with excitement; destroy them with effort ("How could this be wrong?"). The few that survive are the diamonds.
